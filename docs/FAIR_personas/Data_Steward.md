# 	Data_Steward	
[Data_Steward](Data_Steward.md)		
=== "Description"
    Data stewardship encompasses the set of practices that ensure data is properly collected, stored, maintained, and shared throughout its entire lifecycle. FAIR data stewardship extends traditional data management by introducing a coordinated framework that ensures data is (or becomes) and remains compliant to Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable (FAIR) principles.
    The core responsibilities of a Data Steward vary widely, ranging from policy development and advisory functions to operational, technical, and IT-related activities. These responsibilities may differ across research-performing and data-driven organizations, where job titles and scopes of work may also vary. Data stewardship roles can be coordination-focused, policy-oriented, research-oriented, or infrastructure-oriented, depending on organizational needs and maturity. A Data Steward may also serve as a lead role within a data governance initiative, taking ownership of data assets and collaborating with business and technical stakeholders to define and implement governance objectives. This role is closely linked to maintaining data integrity and control within data governance and master data management frameworks.

=== "Persona tasks"
    A FAIR Data Steward serves as a connector between scientific, regulatory, business, and technical functions, ensuring that data remains accurate, compliant, and aligned with both organizational and FAIR principles. In the pharmaceutical context, the role underpins data integrity and regulatory compliance while enabling data reuse across R&D, manufacturing, and post-market activities for example. The FAIR Data Steward may help define and enforces governance policies, establish and monitor data quality standards, and ensures that metadata and provenance information are complete, standardized, and persistently linked to underlying datasets.

    This role requires continuous collaboration with multiple stakeholders. Working closely with the Master Data Manager and Reference Data Manager, the steward ensures harmonized and trusted master and reference data. In partnership with the Data Owner and Data Protection Officer, they clarify accountability and ensure adherence to privacy and data protection regulations. Together with the Data Quality Manager and Subject Matter Experts, they validate data definitions, ensure high-quality standards, and maintain contextual relevance. Coordination with the Data Standards and Governance Expert and the Data Integration Specialist guarantees alignment with enterprise standards and seamless interoperability across systems. Engagement with the FAIR Community Manager, Curator, and Ontologist supports consistent application of semantic models, promotes best practices, and strengthens the organization’s FAIR culture.

    Through these collaborations, the FAIR Data Steward acts as an integrator who embeds FAIR principles into daily operations, transforming data into a trusted, reusable, and compliant asset. This enables the organization to achieve greater efficiency, regulatory readiness, and scientific and business value throughout the pharmaceutical data lifecycle. They can also act as a link between business and technical teams, ensuring data is accurate, compliant, and aligned with organizational goals.
    They define governance policies, procedures, and roles, set and maintain data quality rules, monitor KPIs, and resolve issues with relevant stakeholders. They may (alone or in coordination with other FAIR personas) manage master data to maintain a single source of truth, ensure proper integration and synchronization, and keep metadata and data catalogs accurate and accessible.	


=== "Upside and Downside"
    ### Upside
    Implementing FAIR principles would reduce these challenges and unlock efficiency, compliance, and reuse.		

    ### Downside
    Typical issues for a Data Steward include unclear or overlapping role definitions, leading to confusion or gaps in data stewardship and governance. They often struggle with poor data quality and incomplete or inconsistent definitions, which undermine trust and value in organizational data. Cultural resistance—where colleagues see new governance practices as bureaucratic or disruptive—can make adoption challenging. Data stewards must also navigate increasing complexity in regulatory compliance, data security, and integration of new technologies. Finally, many face resource constraints, such as lack of time, training, or effective tools, reducing their overall impact.


=== "Benefits"
    The adoption of FAIR principles enhances data quality, trust, and discoverability while minimizing duplication of effort. FAIR provides a structured framework. By improving data quality, consistency, and documentation, it rebuilds trust in organizational data and ensures a reliable foundation for analytics and decision-making. FAIR’s emphasis on metadata, accessibility, and interoperability supports stronger compliance with regulatory frameworks, simplifies auditability, and streamlines integration across systems. Moreover, it helps overcome cultural resistance by embedding transparent, value-driven practices that demonstrate efficiency and impact. Ultimately, FAIR empowers Data Stewards to achieve greater operational efficiency, foster collaboration, and enable sustainable data reuse across the organization.
